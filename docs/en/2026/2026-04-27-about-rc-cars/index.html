Arrma Gorgon: Purchase, Repairs and Costs

>>>
April 27, 2026  |  Kategorie: blog
Table of Contents
  • Conclusion

And now to something completely different…

A few weeks ago I dove into a new “hobby”: radio-controlled semi-pro vehicles — a birthday present for my kid.

So I threw myself into the research. For me, robustness and modularity were the deciding factors. I wanted my kid to be able to plow through rough terrain with the rig, and I knew that things would break now and then. So good spare-part availability was important.

I eventually came across the Arrma Gorgon — a 1/10 monster truck. Price new, without battery and charger: 180 EUR. Not cheap, but fair. And because driving together is twice the fun, I bought two vehicles. I also got four lithium-polymer batteries. That’s where the whole compatibility madness starts: LiPo batteries require a specific charger. Since the stock vehicle uses brushed motors, I also bought two fans so I wouldn’t have to take a forced break after 20 minutes of driving. And since the fans were shipped without screws, I had to buy a 500-piece RC screw kit. You see — it adds up… but there’s more.

Much more. For example, the fan connector didn’t fit the controller. So I took out the side cutters and trimmed the protruding bit of plastic. Great start for modularity.

Here is the initial (!) shopping list:

The first day was a total success. The electric motors have plenty of punch and we had an insane amount of fun. But already on the first day the first disappointment happened: the front sway bar broke — the rod that guides the front suspension.

I was able to temporarily repair the shock with a suitable hose coupling until the new set of shocks arrived. Fixed professionally with duct tape, naturally.

Costs for that:

Qty Item Price
1 Shock set 11mm bore, 116mm length, 500cSt oil (ARA330756) 23,30 EUR
1 Shipping 3,90 EUR

The next day the steering failed. At first it’s incredibly confusing because you don’t immediately know what’s broken. Eventually you find the servo motor for the steering — which failed on both vehicles on day two.

From my perspective the problem is: the shock absorber may protect the body, but not the tires. These are simple tubeless, very soft rubber tires. Every collision transmits forces directly into the control arms, the steering and the suspension. And the servos use cheap plastic horns that obviously don’t last long. It’s basically a design flaw… but apparently I’m the first person to notice?

So out came the craft scissors and I cut a piece of HD‑PE pipe and bolted it to the small shock absorber.

And I ordered a new set of servos.

Costs for that:

Qty Item Price
1 Miuzei digital servo 25kg 270° (pair) for mini RC car 1/8 1/10 1/12 24,98 EUR

The installation is quite complicated because the control arms sit awkwardly under a plastic bridge. Eventually it worked and we were ready to go again…

If only the problems would stop there.

Within less than a week the drive motors gave up. Troubleshooting took many hours. Of course you don’t expect motors to die after one week — I even installed fans to protect the sensitive parts.

Eventually it became clear: both motors were dead. Broken. Useless. I had had enough and contacted rc-fox support.

They were polite and quickly offered replacements for both motors. Not wanting a fight, I ignored the broken shock and the servos and accepted the new motors — I just wanted to drive!

When the motors arrived I installed them immediately. Everything went smoothly. Full of anticipation we took the cars out again.

After a few minutes, another shock: one car started emitting thick smoke. This time it was user error — in my excitement I had forgotten to put the insulation back on the connection between motor and controller. Short circuit.

And a short circuit takes out several components, because apparently there’s no fuse anywhere.

Well, fine — I had planned to switch to brushless motors anyway. So the research machine got going again. And here’s a small but important tip: don’t blindly trust Gemini & ChatGPT for compatibility — they’ll keep recommending stuff that doesn’t actually fit. Compatibility seems to be something these AIs gloss over. That led to the next problem:

I found what looked like the perfect brushless motor. Of course it doesn’t come alone: you also need a new controller and a receiver. The stock receiver is integrated, and the new receiver came with — nothing. No mounting options. Details…

Before installing the motor I wanted to test it: controller and motor connected, controller plugged in and battery attached.

The battery attached? Think again. The battery came with an IC3 connector. It has two main poles and a small pin that lets the charger read capacity and status. High tech! The controller, however, had an EC3 connector — they don’t fit.

I searched and ordered an adapter cable.

Qty Item Price
1 EC3 male to XT60 adapter with 5cm 14awg wire for RC LiPo battery 8,99 EUR

Adapter arrived, I plugged everything in — controller, battery — motor runs.

So far so good. Then I tried to fit the new motor into the housing. Well… I tried. The original motor has rear-exit wires; the new motor has side-exit wiring. Also the new motor is much shorter.

Short: it doesn’t fit into the housing. Frustration sets in.

Costs for the first motor — which I promptly returned:

Qty Item Price
1 Quicrun brushless combo WP10BL120G2 with 3652SL-3250KV-G2 for 1:10 84,95 EUR
1 SLR300 3-channel SLT receiver (single protocol) 27,48 EUR
1 Shipping 5,99 EUR

I emailed the seller and returned the motor. The search continued until I found a longer motor that might fit.

Costs for the longer motor:

Qty Item Price
1 Quicrun brushless combo WP10BL120G2 with 3660SL-3150KV-G2 (HW38030210) 82,75 EUR
1 Shipping 5,90 EUR

A few days pass. The combo arrives. Excited, I unpack it.

The motor actually fits the housing. So I dug out the old pinion and… hold on — something still doesn’t fit.

While the pinion fit the “short” motor without issue, the longer motor has a thicker shaft: 5 mm. The old pinion, however, has a 3,175 mm bore.

Back to searching. I found a promising pinion on Amazon to save on shipping.

Costs:

Qty Item Price
1 13T Mod1 Safe-D5 pinion 9,99 EUR

I put the pinion on, tightened it up.

But it doesn’t fit. The teeth barely mesh with the gearbox gear and the diameter is too large. Friction would be too high. And since the big gearbox gear is just plastic, it probably wouldn’t last long.

Anger. Frustration. Irritation. Self-doubt. The search continues, because the next problem now is that the right pinion should not have more than 12 teeth, the tooth module must not exceed 0.8, and the shaft is D-shaped (Safe‑D) — not round. Those are details you need to know, and they narrow the choices a lot. I decided to skip the D‑shape and hope that a simple 5 mm bore pinion with 12 teeth and module 0.8 will do.

Qty Item Price
1 5‑piece hardened steel pinion set for 5 mm shafts — 11T, 12T, 13T, 14T, 15T 15,99 EUR

Conclusion

This conclusion is preliminary — I’m hoping it’s just the wrong pinion and that no further surprises are waiting for me.

First: self-criticism — the short circuit is on me. That was seriously dumb.

༼ʘ̚ ʖ ʘ̚༽

And yes, the technical properties of the components — once you know what to look for — aren’t actually that hard to understand. You just need to know where to look. Call it learning costs or tuition. At least one thing I gained from the whole affair: tinkering with the hardware is a lot of fun.

But then there’s the quality. The shock absorber that breaks after maybe three hours. The electric motors exposed to dirt and dust with no protection. The servos that died after two days. The tiny bumper. From my point of view the system simply isn’t designed for hard off‑road use. But that’s not necessarily the impression you get from the promo videos:

https://www.arrma-rc.com/de/product/1-10-gorgon-2wd-rtr-brushed-monster-truck-blue/ARA3230T1.html

And in that light, the explanation “you should have researched better” doesn’t quite cut it anymore, does it?
