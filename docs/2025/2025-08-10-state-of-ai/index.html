State Of AI: Year 3 of the hype. Things I learned.

Coding with AI

People don’t get tired announcing that AI will replace developers. Mostly salespeoplez. Microsoft claims that AI is already writing 30–50% of their code. But they slient about the detailös. I am not totally disagreeing, but I am pretty sure that there’s still a bumpy road ahead. You may argue: Well, then you don’t know how to proper use AI. Problem sits infront of the screen, as they say. Fair engough. That’s why I want to share some thoughts and experiences.

AI is increasing my efficiency to a very high degree. But also it requires good skills as a developer. And if you don’t keep your head up, you loose more time with “playing around” than wining by AI.

Speaking of plyaing around: These days I was just doing that. Ai enables me to do this more frequently. It’s so much easiere to wrap you head around rroegin ground. OK. This is clearly not about coding, but it's making point. I am not blaming AI for not being able to create a simple bike SVG. Especially because SVG creation is not really something a developer does. But it's a perfect metaphor for how the process goes sideways:

AI bots have a over-confident way of approaching things. They always try to flatter the user (I learned this wonderful word “sycophancy” these days – and forgot it the next day). And thhey rotate around problems. 

While they are perfect in solving "cllosed and recurring tasks", they suffer from the "open-ended" problem. Microsoft just published a study about jobs that are most pürobably being replaced by AI. And most of them are exactly this: Translator and interpretors. Perfect tasks for AI. Modesl! Can be replaced by generative AI. Salespeopel - easy, just train a model on product data and you are good. And so on. But a good developer has something that AI does not have: Creativiy and forseeablity. 
 
![alt text](image-10.png)

If you are into troubleshooting or exploring, you may have experienced this: You ask the AI to fix a problem or explain how to achieve something. It will provide you with a solution. Which is usually quite overwhelming. You start implementing this "pamphlet" from step 1 to step 20 or so and at step 5 you get stuck.

The system you are working on returns an error. We can't blame the AI, because the reason is usually: You did not provide enough context about the environment and circumstances! But it’s also important to say: How do you know what details are missing?

You can't. Especially in troubleshooting, it's part of the process. The process!

The process of troubleshooting is not having a page of steps to follow and after 10 minutes you are done. If you are a system administrator, you know what troubleshooting is. It’s a forth and back, trial-and-error process. You get better with time, that's what a good, seasoned, senior system administrator is: Someone who has seen a lot of problems and knows how to approach them.

AI has seen a lot of problems. They are trained on a lot of problems. Still, what they haven’t learned yet — none of them — is: guiding you through processes step by step. Or at least offering it! Instead: wasting 1,000 tokens on a 20-step solution.

You may think it’s part of the business model: Why should the AI bot efficiently guide you through a process, if it can provide you with a 20-step solution, which won’t work and then: Your context window is out of tokens. Purchase the pro plan. Wait 6 hours to go on.

So, one word of advice: End every prompt like this. It will save you a lot of time and tokens:

    "Guide me through the process step by step, don’t answer all at once, but wait for my confirmation to continue."

But what about coding, at all? There are so many platforms offering perfect low-code or even no-code solutions. People telling you they built whole businesses without writing a single line of code.

You tried it yourself? Try.

I am not saying that AI is not capable of e.g. writing code. It is!

But "real apps" are complex. They are not just an HTML one-pager with a nice parallax effect, some neat JavaScript interactions and CSS. There are backends, data models, APIs, authentication, security, data storage and so on.

Again, these days I was writing this little app that ought to visualize biking trips on a map. It grew a little out of hand. Starting with a Jupyter notebook, I ended up with a full-blown web app. And you know what? I did it almost the "vibe coding" way. I only spent time thinking about user experience, features, bugs.

And at some point I thought: "Hey buddy, what you actually did there under the hood?"

I was using an extensive pre-prompt instructions file, telling the agent to use a structure of separated files, speaking function names and so on. Still I was facing a huge code base. The main app file was over 5,000 lines of code. I reached a limit, where even the agent wasn’t really able to process all the context there. It was a mess.

Well, use the agent to refactor, you are saying?

I did. And it didn’t really work out. It was creating stubs and redundant functions and endpoints.

The end of the story: I had to crawl through the code myself. And it was not a difficult thing, I just spent two or three hours or so to identify redundant code, moved it out of the main function, cleaned up file structure. With the help of AI, but only as a sidekick.

And this is what I am saying: You still need to be a good developer to understand code architecture and how code works. AI will not be able to deliver a fully fledged market-ready app for you.

However, this is an article about "things I learned", so let’s summarize:

    always instruct something like “step by step” in your prompts

    use a pre-prompt instructions file to guide the agent

    split the work: use an agent to get the pre-prompt instructions, let an agent build you the first starting prompt, and so on

    I learned that it is really helpful when the agent maintains a “protocol” that contains the initial task and then, in very short bullet points, the current status of the task – this way you can always ask the agent to continue with the last task without having to re-explain everything

    also separate tasks and codebases, to keep the context window small – this is the rise of the microservices-ish architecture, a “monolithic” app can't be eaten by the agent


## What about "non coding"?

Do you remember, back in the days, when you were "googlin" for information? First people start looking for the whole term, like "how to build a web app with AI". But the Google algorithm was improving and so does the prompts. People reduced their search terms to "web app AI". You don't necessriyl need fillwords to understand the intention of a search. Not only fillwords, also spelling or grammar.

Right now the same happens to AI, apparently. Sometimes i see myself typing prompts with so many errors and grammar. But it's doing it's job. I wonder if this leads to "degration" or better "optimisation" of our language. Have you heard of [Gibberlink](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gibberlink)? It's a language that is used by AI agents to communicate with each other. It is a simplified version of English, without all the "unnecessary" words. As we are talking to AI more and more, what if, at some point, we adapt our language to the AI?

Second thing that will change, is probalby how we will use our "computers". There are not many user interfaces right now. We have the GUI - the graphiocal user interface, it's the windows and buttons you see on the screen. You can touch them o use your mouse cursor to select text. We have the TUI - text user interface, whjich is most common for CLI (command line interface) applications. You can type commands and see the output in text form. And we have the VUI - voice user interface, which is becoming more and more common with the rise of smart speakers and voice assistants. All of them have there strengths and weaknesses. Building a powerpoint app wioth a TUI is probalbvy quite difficult. Grepping through multiple servers request log using voioce or a moues? no one does it. 

Right now, we are using AI in two ways: as a VUI and a TUI. Ther's the MCP (Model Context Protocol), which conects the agent to other systems, like - sticking with this example - powerpoint.
